UI Design Mobile 2025

Bloom — Wellness App UI

UI design for a daily mental wellness app, with a focus on creating a calm, low-friction experience that fits into a user's morning routine.

Year

2025

Role

UI Designer

Tools

Figma, Mobbin

Overview

Bloom is a concept app for daily mental wellness check-ins. The idea came from noticing that most wellness apps feel either too clinical or too gamified — they either look like a medical form or try too hard to be fun in a way that feels forced.

The design goal was to create something that felt genuinely calm — something you'd actually want to open first thing in the morning. That meant making deliberate choices about colour, typography, spacing, and the way interactions work.

Process

01

Mood Board & Direction

Collected references on Mobbin and Pinterest, focusing on apps with soft, warm palettes and generous whitespace. Decided early on to avoid any harsh contrasts or bright accent colours.

02

Core Screens

Designed five key screens: onboarding, daily check-in, mood log history, a breathing exercise, and a simple settings page. Kept the navigation minimal — a bottom bar with just three tabs.

03

Typography & Colour

Used a warm off-white background instead of pure white, and a muted sage green as the main accent. The heading font is slightly rounded, which softens the overall feel of the interface.

04

Accessibility Check

Ran all colour combinations through a contrast checker. A few of the original muted tones didn't pass AA — adjusted them slightly to meet the standard without losing the calm aesthetic.

Outcome

The final design covers the full onboarding flow and the five core screens. This was mainly a visual design exercise rather than a full UX case study, so there was no user testing — but it was a useful project for practising mobile-specific design decisions like touch target sizes and thumb zone layouts.

The biggest design lesson from Bloom was about restraint. Every time I wanted to add another element or another colour, holding back usually produced a better result. The screen that took the longest to get right was the daily check-in — the challenge was making it feel meaningful without making it feel like a form.
